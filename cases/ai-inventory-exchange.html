The Problem

Every distributor was an island

Here's the situation I walked into: TVS Motors has 90+ distributors spread across India, each managing their own inventory independently. Spare parts that were piling up in a Chennai warehouse for 12-18 months were the exact same parts that a Hyderabad distributor was losing service appointments over because they couldn't get stock.

The total dead stock across the network was sitting at 18% of total inventory. That's crores of rupees locked in warehouse shelves, depreciating every quarter, with no systematic way to move it.

Distributors tried the obvious fixes: deep discounts to local retailers, return requests to TVS. None of it worked at scale because the core problem wasn't pricing or returns.

The Real Insight

The problem wasn't inventory. It was information asymmetry. Distributor A had surplus of a part that Distributor B urgently needed, but neither knew about the other's situation. Every distributor was making local decisions without network-level intelligence.

Discovery & Research

I went to the warehouses myself

Before writing a single PRD, I visited 12+ distributor warehouses across South India. I needed to understand the problem from the ground, not from dashboards.

What I found: rows of shelving units filled with parts that hadn't moved in over a year. Warehouse managers who knew exactly which parts were dead but had no way to do anything about it. Regional sales teams who would informally call each other asking "do you have Part X?" but with no systematic process.

The informal phone calls were the biggest signal. Distributors were already trying to solve this problem manually. That told me the demand for cross-network trading was real. They just needed the infrastructure.

The Key Decision

Three models. One clear winner.

After defining the problem, I explored three fundamentally different approaches. This wasn't about features. It was about choosing the right business model that could scale across 90+ distributors.

ApproachHow It WorksProsConsVerdict
Central BuybackTVS buys back dead stock from distributors, redistributesSimple for distributorsTVS absorbs all inventory risk. Capital intensive. Doesn't scale.✗
Manual MatchingRegional teams coordinate transfers via calls/emailsNo tech investmentAlready failing. Can't scale. No pricing logic. No data capture.✗
AI-Powered MarketplaceDistributors trade directly; AI handles matching, pricing, logisticsSelf-sustaining. Network effects. Data flywheel. TVS provides intelligence, not capital.Requires trust-building. Cold start problem.✓ Selected
Why marketplace over central buyback?
A buyback model requires TVS to take on inventory risk and deploy capital. The marketplace creates a self-sustaining ecosystem where distributors trade directly. TVS provides the intelligence layer, not the capital. This means the system gets better with scale instead of more expensive.
How did you solve the "why help my competitor" problem?
Distributors are competitive by nature. We implemented anonymous listings with quality guarantees. Sellers don't know who's buying, buyers don't know who's selling. The marketplace is the trusted intermediary. This was non-negotiable for adoption.
What was your pricing philosophy?
The discount engine balances three forces: seller's holding cost (incentive to sell), buyer's demand urgency (willingness to pay), and network-level price consistency (brand protection). We optimized for transaction velocity, not margin maximization.
How did you handle the cold-start problem?
Started with 8 distributors in South India where we had the strongest relationships. Seeded the marketplace with the highest-value dead stock items first. Early wins created word-of-mouth that drove organic expansion.

Impact & Results

Locked capital became working capital

  • Crores locked in parts aging 6-18 months
  • Distributors losing service revenue from stockouts
  • Zero cross-network inventory visibility
  • Manual phone calls as the only matching mechanism

The Flywheel Effect

This isn't just an inventory tool. Every transaction generates data that improves demand prediction, informs procurement strategy, and strengthens the distributor ecosystem. The marketplace gets smarter with every trade. Dead stock data feeds back into demand forecasting, reducing future dead stock creation.

Reflections

What I learned

What Worked

Field visits before PRD. Understanding the informal phone-call workaround gave us conviction that demand was real. Anonymous listings removed the biggest adoption barrier. Starting with 8 distributors let us iterate fast.

What I'd Do Differently

I'd invest more in the logistics integration earlier. We underestimated how much transfer friction (shipping time, damage risk) would suppress transaction rates in the first month. The intelligence layer was ready before the physical infrastructure.

Open Questions

Can this model extend beyond spare parts to finished goods? What's the optimal network density before marketplace effects kick in? Should pricing be fully algorithmic or leave room for negotiation?
